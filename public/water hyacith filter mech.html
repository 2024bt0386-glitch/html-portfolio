Mechanism of Microplastic Removal by Water Hyacinth

Water hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes) removes microplastics primarily through root adsorption, achieving 55-78% efficiency for 0.5-2 μm polystyrene particles within 48 hours to 5 days, with adsorption dominating over minimal absorption (22-105 times higher).

Root-Specific Adsorption Process

The roots feature extremely dense lateral roots and abundant root caps with a total surface area exceeding 150,000 mm² per plant, serving as primary entrapment sites for microplastics via physical adhesion to mucilage-covered surfaces and root hairs.

Fluorescence microscopy confirms microplastics accumulate on root caps, where microscopic structures like rhizoids and sloughing epidermal cells enhance particle capture through Van der Waals forces and electrostatic interactions, preventing free movement in water.

Quantitative assays show adsorption capacities of 1,060-1,426 μg/g fresh weight for different sizes, with smaller particles (0.5 μm) showing slightly lower rates due to diffusion limits but overall rapid surface trapping.

Anatomical Barriers and Resilience

A unique "vascular ring" in the stem blocks microplastic translocation to aerial tissues, confining particles to roots and preserving leaves for safe harvesting or secondary uses like biogas.This structure, combined with root cap renewal (constant sloughing), maintains plant health without physiological stress, enabling sustained remediation in contaminated waters up to 50 mg/L. Overall, the mechanism leverages passive physical filtration over active uptake, making water hyacinth resilient and scalable for phytoremediation.

Quantitative Removal Efficiency

Water hyacinth achieves rapid microplastic removal, with 48-hour efficiencies of 55.3% for 0.5 μm, 69.1% for 1 μm, and 68.8% for 2 μm polystyrene particles at 50 mg/L concentration, rising to 62.8%, 78.3%, and 76.8% after 5 days across seven replicates. Adsorption dominates, contributing 1,060–1,426 μg/g fresh root weight, while absorption remains minimal (22–105 times lower), confirmed by fluorescence assays separating surface elution from internal homogenization. Smaller particles show slightly lower rates due to diffusion barriers but consistent surface trapping, outperforming prior studies like Yang et al.'s 66.4% for 20 nm and 44.7% for 200 nm nanoparticles.

Microstructural Root Features

Roots develop extremely dense lateral roots (surface area >150,000 mm²/plant) covered in mucilage, root hairs, and rhizoids, enabling physical entrapment via Van der Waals forces, electrostatic adhesion, and mechanical sieving on sloughing epidermal cells. Fluorescence microscopy reveals particle accumulation exclusively on root caps, with no translocation beyond the root endodermis due to a protective vascular ring in the stem, preserving aerial tissues uncontaminated.
